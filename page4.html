Openmaking

PROS and CONS

Openmaking

Open making movement, which refers to the trend of open-sourcing designs for physical objects and encouraging people to make them on their own, has gained momentum in recent years. One notable example is Opendesk, an open making platform that allows designers to share their furniture designs with the public, who can then download the digital files and produce the pieces themselves. While this movement certainly has its benefits, there are also some drawbacks to consider.

Pros

Accessibility: One of the primary benefits of the open making movement is that it makes well-designed, high-quality furniture more accessible to a wider audience. Instead of needing to purchase expensive furniture from high-end retailers, people can simply download the design files and have the pieces made locally or even make them themselves.

Customization: Because the designs are open-source, people are free to modify them to better fit their needs. This means that the furniture can be customized to fit different spaces, accommodate different body types, or even be made from different materials.

Sustainability: Open making can be more environmentally friendly than mass production. By allowing people to produce furniture locally, it reduces the carbon footprint associated with shipping and reduces the waste associated with large-scale manufacturing.

Community building: The open making movement can also help to build communities of makers who share a passion for creating things. By sharing designs and collaborating on projects, people can connect with one another and build relationships around a shared interest.

Cons

Skill level required: While open making platforms like Opendesk make it easier to produce furniture, it still requires a certain level of skill to produce high-quality pieces. This can be a barrier for some people who don't have the tools or expertise necessary to make the furniture on their own.

Quality control: Because anyone can download and produce the designs, there is a risk that the quality of the finished product may vary. This can be especially problematic if the design is not properly tested or if the person producing the piece doesn't have the necessary skill or tools.

Intellectual property: Open-source designs are often licensed under Creative Commons, which allows people to modify and distribute the designs freely. This can be problematic for designers who want to protect their intellectual property or make a living from their designs.

Limited variety: While there are many designs available on open making platforms like Opendesk, the variety of designs is still somewhat limited compared to what is available from traditional furniture retailers. This can make it difficult to find a piece that fits a specific aesthetic or need.
In conclusion, while the open making movement has many benefits, there are also some drawbacks to consider.

Conclutions

The accessibility, customization, sustainability, and community-building aspects are certainly attractive, but the skill level required, quality control issues, intellectual property concerns, and limited variety may be significant barriers for some. Overall, the open making movement is a fascinating development in the world of design and manufacturing, and it will be interesting to see how it evolves in the coming years.
